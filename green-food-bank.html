Identifying Food Bank's Service Gap Through Ethnographic Research

Client: Oasis Green Food Bank
Role: Research Apprentice
Skills: Service Design, Research Design, Qualitative Research

Context: In 2016, Oasis Green Food Bank of Shanghai became the first food bank in mainland China. Withithout first-hand research, however, Oasis found it challenging to comprehensively identify food bank beneficiaries and their needs. I was part of an ambitious project to understand the living conditions of the urban poor and their need for food assistance in Shanghai.

Process: We conducted ethnographic research using a variety of research methodologies, including semi-structured in-depth interview, non-participant observation, and questionnaires. The team identified 5 key populations at risk of food shortage, and mapped them to a beneficiary classification pyramid.

Results: The model was immediately adopted by the client and informed its subsequent expansion of operations nationwide.

Secondary research helped construct representative samples for user interviews.

Scientific Research Design

Before going into the field, we spent a week researching and understanding the problem landscape and reviewing the existing literature.

Through reviewing academic research on food bank beneficiaries, annual reports of Social Welfare Ministry, census data on migrant workers, and news reports on urban poverty in Shanghai, we identified 3 major categories of potential food bank beneficiaries, and broke down those categories even further to ensure there is plenty of within-category variance.

The purple dots on the left indicate groups that we found through research to be the most vulnerable to food shortages.

Ethnographic research discovered unexpected preferences for food assistance, and identified common causes for food insecurity.

Research findings

Through semi-structured in-depth interviews, we discovered interesting demands and preferences of food bank beneficiaries. For instance, a family with a newborn baby spoke passionately against baby formulas donations. We used user profiles to capture surprising findings.

We also discovered a common trend that those who have the most dire needs for food assistance often suffer from health conditions and/or major accidents.

To synthesize research insights, we modified Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs to create a conceptual model for classifying food bank beneficiaries.

Synthesis and Ideation

Households and individuals experiencing major accidents and crises are considered to be on the 5th ladder - they are often in dire need of food assistance. Those on social security may not have easy access to fruits, meats, and diaries, and are considered to be on the 4th ladder - they constitute the majority of our interviewees. Some current food bank beneficiaries already have consistent access to fruits, etc; they complained about the quality of the food donations. They are considered to be on the 3rd ladder.

We recommended that the Food Bank focus on servicing those on the 4th and 5th ladder.

Our research insights helped expand the non-profit’s services both locally and nationally.

Project impact

Our research result, the beneficiary classification pyramid, made its way to the client’s website, demonstrating the fact that the Food Bank has recognized our research and employed the insights into their daily practice.

In the three years following our project, Green Food Bank expanded from a local organization serving 2 neighborhoods, to a national organization with 20 locations in Shanghai alone. It has expanded to reach 9 other provinces, developing a network of food banks across China. In 2018 alone, it provided food assistance to over 430K people.
